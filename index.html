How I Work

I follow the Design Thinking framework — the human-centered approach developed at Stanford's d.school. But frameworks are just scaffolding. What matters is how you bring them to life. Here's how I do it.

1

Empathize

I start by getting curious. Who are we designing for? What do they actually need — not what we assume they need? I conduct research, run interviews, and sit with the discomfort of not knowing. The answers aren't on the surface. You have to listen for what's said and unsaid, done and not done.

2

Define

Research generates a lot of data. My job is to find the signal in the noise — the insight that reframes the challenge. Sometimes the problem you were asked to solve isn't the problem worth solving. This phase is about synthesizing what I've learned into a clear point of view.

3

Ideate

With a clear problem, I generate possibilities. Lots of them. I push past the obvious ideas to find the ones that might actually change something. I'm not precious here — the goal is divergent thinking, not perfection.

4

Prototype

Ideas need form. I sketch, I build, I make things tangible — quickly and roughly at first. Prototypes aren't about proving I'm right. They're about learning what's wrong so I can make it better.

5

Test

I put prototypes in front of real people and watch what happens. Not to validate — to learn. Testing is another opportunity to empathize, to refine the problem, to discover something I missed. Then we iterate.

Design thinking isn't linear. I move between phases as the work demands — sometimes looping back to empathize after testing, sometimes redefining the problem mid-prototype. The best insights often come from going back.
